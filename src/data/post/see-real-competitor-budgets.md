## Why Competitor Budgets Matter More Than Creatives

Most e-commerce sellers obsess over competitor ad creatives — what images they use, what hooks they test, what angles they run. Creatives are visible. Anyone can open the Meta Ad Library and see what a competitor is running.

But creatives only tell you *what* a competitor is testing. They do not tell you *whether it is working*.

Ad spend tells you whether it is working.

A competitor might run 50 different ad creatives. But if 45 of them have minimal spend and only 5 are getting real budget, those 5 are the ones generating revenue. The rest are either tests that failed or legacy ads still technically "active" but starving.

If you can see real competitor budgets — not just which ads exist, but which ads are getting money — you can separate signal from noise. You can identify the proven winners instead of guessing. And you can benchmark your own spend against competitors in your niche to know whether you are under-investing, over-investing, or right in line.

This guide explains how to see real competitor budgets in 2026, what data is actually available, and how to turn it into actionable strategy.

## The Problem: Why "Ad Spend" Has Always Been a Black Box

Historically, competitor ad spend was the holy grail of competitive intelligence — and it was almost impossible to get.

Ad platforms do not show you how much a specific advertiser is spending. The Meta Ad Library, for example, shows you which ads are active and when they started, but it does not show spend. You could see that a competitor has 200 active ads, but you had no idea whether they were spending $500/day or $50,000/day.

Third-party tools like AdSpy, BigSpy, and PowerAdSpy indexed ad creatives but provided spend estimates that were essentially guesses — extrapolated from how frequently the tool's crawlers saw the ad. These estimates were directionally useful at best and wildly inaccurate at worst.

This created a fundamental asymmetry: you could see *what* competitors were doing, but not *how seriously* they were doing it. A competitor running an ad for 90 days could be spending $50/day or $5,000/day. The creative looks the same either way. The strategic implication is completely different.

## How EU/UK Transparency Rules Changed the Game

In 2023, the European Union's Digital Services Act (DSA) went into effect, requiring large online platforms — including Meta (Facebook/Instagram) — to publish detailed information about ads running in the EU/UK market.

This includes:

- **Ad creative** (the actual image, video, or text)
- **Ad start and end dates**
- **Advertiser identity** (name and verified status)
- **Targeting parameters** (age, gender, location, interests)
- **Total impressions** (aggregate number of times the ad was shown in the EU/UK)
- **Budget/spend ranges** (binned categories, not exact dollar amounts)

This data is publicly accessible through the Meta Ad Library API — but it is raw, unstructured, and impractical to use manually at scale. The API returns individual ad records, and to build a picture of a competitor's total spend, you would need to query every single ad, aggregate the impression data, and cross-reference spend ranges across hundreds or thousands of ads.

This is where tools like TrendTrack add value: they ingest this data, aggregate it, and present it in a usable format.

## What You Can Actually See (and What You Cannot)

Before diving into strategy, it is important to understand the limitations of transparency data.

### What You CAN See

| Data Point | Source | What It Tells You |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| Total impressions per ad | Meta Ad Library (EU/UK) | How many times each ad was shown in the EU/UK market |
| Impression ranges | Meta Ad Library (EU/UK) | Binned categories (e.g., "200K–500K", "500K–1M", "1M+") |
| Ad duration | Meta Ad Library | How long each ad has been running |
| Number of active ads | Meta Ad Library | How many creatives a competitor is currently testing |
| Targeting parameters | Meta Ad Library (EU/UK) | Who the competitor is targeting (age, gender, location, interests) |
| Creative variations | Meta Ad Library | All ad formats and copy variations being tested |

### What You CANNOT See

| Data Point | Why Not |
|-----------|---------|
| Exact dollar spend | Meta only provides binned ranges, not exact amounts |
| Global spend (outside EU/UK) | DSA only covers EU/UK impressions; US and other markets are not included |
| ROAS or conversion data | No platform reveals advertiser-side conversion metrics |
| Revenue per ad | Not available from any public source |
| Profitability | You can infer whether an ad is likely profitable (if it runs for months with increasing spend), but you cannot confirm it |

The key insight: transparency data gives you **relative spend signals**, not exact budgets. A competitor with 1M+ EU/UK impressions on a single ad is spending significantly more than one with 10K–50K impressions. The exact dollar amounts are hidden, but the relative comparison is reliable.

## How to Read Impression Data as Budget Signals

Since you cannot see exact dollar amounts, you need to interpret impression data as a proxy for ad spend. Here is how:

### Impression Tiers and What They Mean

TrendTrack categorizes ads into tiers based on EU/UK impression data:

| Impression Range | What It Means | Strategic Signal |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Under 200K | Early testing phase | The ad is new or being tested at low spend. Too early to draw conclusions. |
| 200K–500K | Confirmed test with budget | The advertiser is investing real money. The creative is worth studying. |
| 500K–1M | Validated winner | The ad has been running long enough and getting enough spend to confirm it works. Strong signal. |
| 1M+ | Super creative / scaled winner | This is a proven top performer. The competitor is scaling aggressively. Study everything about this ad. |
| 5M+ | Massive scale | This is a category-defining creative. The competitor is likely spending tens of thousands per day on this single ad. |

### Duration × Impressions: The Winning Formula

A single data point is not enough. You need to combine **duration** (how long the ad has been running) with **impressions** (how much it has been shown):

| Scenario | Duration | Impressions | Interpretation |
|----------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| New + low impressions | < 7 days | < 200K | Just launched. No signal yet. |
| New + high impressions | < 7 days | 500K+ | Aggressive launch. Competitor is spending heavily on day one — likely a tested creative they are scaling fast. |
| Long-running + low impressions | 60+ days | < 200K | Legacy ad. Still technically active but barely spending. Ignore. |
| Long-running + high impressions | 30+ days | 1M+ | **Gold mine.** This is a proven winner that has been scaled. Study the creative, hook, offer, and landing page. |
| Increasing impressions over time | 30+ days | Growing week-over-week | The competitor is scaling this ad. It is working and getting more budget. Strong signal to adapt the angle. |
| Decreasing impressions over time | 30+ days | Declining week-over-week | The ad is fatiguing or being deprioritized. The competitor may be shifting budget to a new creative. |

The most valuable signal is simple: **an ad that has been running for 30+ days with 1M+ impressions and growing spend is almost certainly above the competitor's break-even ROAS.** That is a validated creative angle worth adapting. ## Step-by-Step: How to See Real Competitor Budgets

### Method 1: Manual Meta Ad Library (Free, Limited)

1. Go to [Meta Ad Library](https://www.facebook.com/ads/library/)
2. Search for your competitor's brand name or page
3. Filter by "Active Ads" and country (select an EU country like Germany or France for transparency data)
4. For each ad, check the "Impressions and spend" section — this shows EU/UK impression ranges and date ranges
5. Manually tally up the impression ranges across all active ads to estimate total reach

**Limitations**: Manual, time-consuming, no historical trends, no aggregation. Works for checking one competitor occasionally but does not scale.

## Turning Competitor Budget Data Into Strategy

Seeing the data is only useful if you act on it. Here are five specific strategies:

### Strategy 1: Identify Proven Creative Angles

Find ads with 1M+ impressions that have been running for 30+ days. These are validated winners. Study:

- **The hook** (the first 3 seconds of video or the headline of image ads)
- **The offer** (price, discount, bundle, guarantee)
- **The landing page** (what the user sees after clicking)
- **The target audience** (visible in EU/UK transparency data)

Do not copy the creative. Adapt the angle. If a competitor's winning ad leads with "as seen on TikTok" social proof, test a similar social proof angle with your own product and footage.

### Strategy 2: Benchmark Your Own Spend

If competitors in your niche are running 500K–1M impression ads and you are running 50K–100K, you may be under-investing. This does not mean you should match their spend — but it tells you the competitive floor. If everyone in your category is spending heavily, low-budget testing may not generate enough data to find winners.

Conversely, if competitors are running minimal ads with low impressions, there may be a white space opportunity to outspend and dominate the ad auction.

### Strategy 3: Spot Market Entry and Exit Signals

When a new competitor suddenly appears with high-impression ads (500K+ in the first week), they are making an aggressive market entry. This could signal a well-funded new entrant with tested creatives. Watch them closely.

When an established competitor's total impressions decline sharply week-over-week, they may be pulling back — potentially due to profitability issues, seasonal shifts, or a pivot. This could signal an opportunity to capture their market share.

### Strategy 4: Validate Product Demand Before Launching

Before you launch ads for a new product, check whether competitors are already running scaled ads for similar products. If 3–4 competitors all have 1M+ impression ads in the same product category, demand is validated — but competition is high. If no one is spending meaningfully, either you found a blue ocean or there is no demand. Cross-reference with Google Trends and Amazon search volume to confirm.

### Strategy 5: Time Your Scaling Decisions

When you find a creative that works for you, check whether competitors are also scaling similar angles. If they are, the angle has market-wide momentum — double down. If competitors are pulling back while your creative works, you may have found a unique angle — scale aggressively before they catch on.

## Competitor Budget Intelligence FAQ

**How can I see real competitor budgets?**

In the EU/UK market, the Digital Services Act (DSA) requires Meta to publish impression and spend range data for all ads. You can access this manually through the Meta Ad Library, or use a tool like TrendTrack that aggregates this data automatically with historical trends and impression tier categorization.

**Can I see exact dollar amounts for competitor ad spend?**

No. The DSA requires platforms to publish impression ranges (e.g., "200K–500K") and spend ranges (binned categories), not exact dollar amounts. However, relative comparison between ads and competitors is reliable — an ad with 1M+ impressions is spending significantly more than one with 50K impressions.

**Does the Meta Ad Library show ad spend?**

The Meta Ad Library shows EU/UK impression ranges and spend ranges for ads targeting EU/UK users. It does not show global spend or exact dollar amounts. For ads targeting only US users, no spend or impression data is available.

**What is a good impression count for a winning ad?**

In the EU/UK market, an ad with 500K–1M impressions that has been running for 30+ days is typically a validated winner. Ads with 1M+ impressions are scaled winners — the competitor is investing heavily and the creative is proven. Anything under 200K is still in the testing phase.

**How does TrendTrack get competitor budget data?**

TrendTrack continuously ingests data from the Meta Ad Library API, which provides EU/UK impression and spend range data under DSA transparency requirements. TrendTrack aggregates this data per advertiser, categorizes ads by impression tier, and tracks historical trends — turning raw API data into actionable competitive intelligence.

**Can I see competitor budgets outside the EU/UK?**

Currently, DSA transparency data only covers EU/UK impressions. Ads targeting US, Asia, or other markets do not have public spend data. However, EU/UK impression data is a strong proxy for global strategy — competitors rarely run completely different creative strategies by region.

**How often should I check competitor budgets?**

For active competitors, check weekly. TrendTrack's Brand Tracker (Pro and Business plans) automates this by monitoring competitors daily and alerting you to new ads and impression changes. This way you catch scaling signals in real time instead of discovering them weeks later.

**Is seeing competitor budgets legal?**

Yes. The EU/UK DSA transparency data is publicly available by law. Accessing it through the Meta Ad Library or tools that aggregate public API data is completely legal and within platform terms of service.

**What is the difference between ad impressions and ad spend?**

Impressions measure how many times an ad was shown. Spend measures how much money was paid for those impressions. They are correlated but not identical — cost per impression varies by audience, country, and ad format. Transparency data provides impression ranges (more reliable) rather than exact spend (which is not publicly available).
